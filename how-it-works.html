Chapter 1

The Problem We're Solving

Art Lounge (Platinum Painting Essentials & Trading Pvt. Ltd.) imports art supplies in bulk shipments. A typical import:

Bulk Ordering

You must order 250+ units at a time. Suppliers are overseas. You can't just order 5 tubes of paint when you run low.

Long Lead Times

Sea freight takes 3–6 months. If you realize today you're running low, the new stock won't arrive for months.

Multiple Channels

The same product sells through wholesalers, your own online store (Magento), Amazon, Flipkart, and physical retail. Each drains stock differently.

The Core Question

"For each of my 22,538 products, when will I run out of stock, and how much should I order right now?"

Answering this requires knowing: How much stock do I have? How fast is it selling? Where is it selling? When did I last import? How long until the next shipment arrives?

That's what this system computes — automatically, every single night.

What's a "voucher" in Tally? (click to expand)

▼

A voucher is Tally's word for a transaction record. Think of it as a receipt. Each voucher has:

  • A type — Sales, Purchase, Credit Note, Physical Stock, etc.
  • A party name — who was on the other side (customer or supplier)
  • A date
  • Inventory entries — which products moved, how many, at what price

A single sales voucher might list 15 different products sold to one wholesale customer. Each product line is an "inventory entry."

How does the XML API actually work?

▼

Tally runs an HTTP server on port 9000. Our system sends XML requests, like asking:

// "Give me all stock items with their closing balances" <ENVELOPE> <HEADER><TYPE>Collection</TYPE></HEADER> <BODY> <DESC> <STATICVARIABLES> <SVEXPORTFORMAT>XML</SVEXPORTFORMAT> </STATICVARIABLES> <TDL> <TDLMESSAGE> <COLLECTION NAME="StockItemCollection"> <TYPE>StockItem</TYPE> <FETCH>NAME, CLOSINGBALANCE, CATEGORY</FETCH> </COLLECTION> </TDLMESSAGE> </TDL> </DESC> </BODY> </ENVELOPE>

The full financial year voucher pull is about 190 MB of XML and takes ~110 seconds. This only happens once per nightly sync.

Chapter 4

Party Classification: Who's Buying?

Before we can compute anything useful, we need to know who is buying. A wholesale customer draining 500 units is very different from an online sale of 2 units. The system must separate these channels.

The challenge: Tally doesn't have a "channel" field. We have to figure it out ourselves.

The 7 Channel Types

Wholesale Online Store (Retail) Supplier Internal Ignore Unclassified

The 5-Priority Ruleset

Each transaction is classified using a cascade of 5 rules. The first match wins:

Classification Priority Cascade
Priority Rule Type Example Channel

Why this matters

The channel determines whether a sale counts as demand (wholesale, online, store) or not (internal transfers, physical stock adjustments). Only real demand drives velocity calculations. Internal transfers between your own accounts don't mean people are buying more.

Real example: How "Sales-Tally" is tricky

▼

The voucher type "Sales-Tally" is a catch-all. By analyzing the data, we found:

  • 50% wholesale (various dealer names under Sundry Debtors)
  • 42% online (the party name is "MAGENTO2" — your website)
  • 8% internal (party "Art Lounge India" — inter-company transfer)

This is why the priority cascade is essential. Voucher type alone isn't enough — you need to look at the party name to tell these apart.

Chapter 5

Stock Position Reconstruction

This is the foundation of everything. We need to know: for every product, on every single day of the financial year, how much stock was there?

Tally gives us two anchor points:

Opening Balance

Stock at the start of the financial year (April 1). Tally's authoritative number.

Closing Balance

Stock right now (today). Also from Tally. This is our most trusted number.

Between these two points, we have all the transactions (sales, purchases, adjustments). We "replay" them day by day to fill in the gaps.

The Dual-Anchor Method

We run two reconstructions simultaneously for maximum accuracy:

Interactive Example: Reconstructing 7 Days
Day Opening Purchased Sold Closing In Stock?
In stock (counted for velocity)
Out of stock (excluded from velocity)
Backward Reconstruction
implied_opening = closing_balance - total_inward + total_outward
Start from today's known closing balance and work backwards, subtracting all purchases and adding back all sales
Daily Position (Forward Walk)
closing_today = opening_today + purchased - sold
Each day: start with opening stock, add purchases, subtract sales = closing stock. Tomorrow's opening = today's closing.
Why "In Stock" matters so much

A day is marked in-stock if the closing quantity is > 0 OR if a real sale happened that day (even if the balance went negative). This matters because velocity only counts in-stock days. If an item was out of stock for 3 months, those months don't make the velocity look slower — they're excluded entirely.

Chapter 6

Velocity: How Fast Is It Selling?

Velocity is the heartbeat of the system. It measures how many units of a product sell per day, on average. But not just any average — a smart one.

Velocity Formula
velocity = total_units_sold / in_stock_days
Only days when the product was available count. This gives a true picture of demand.

Why exclude out-of-stock days?

Interactive Example: The Distortion Problem

Imagine a product that sells 1 unit/day when in stock, but was out of stock for 60 days:

Naive Method
0.33
units/day

100 sold / 300 total days. Wrong! It looks like slow demand, but it actually sells fast.

Our Method
1.0
units/day

100 sold / 100 in-stock days. Correct! Reflects actual demand.

Three Velocity Channels

Velocity is calculated separately for each sales channel:

Wholesale

Bulk B2B orders. Usually large, infrequent spikes.

Online

Magento, Amazon, Flipkart. Small, steady trickle.

Store

Physical retail counter. Walk-in customers.

Total velocity = wholesale + online + store (per day).

Flat vs. Weighted Moving Average (WMA)

The flat velocity averages sales over the entire financial year (all in-stock days). It gives the big-picture average.

flat_velocity = total_sold_FY / total_in_stock_days_FY

Good for: stable products with consistent demand patterns.

The WMA velocity only looks at the last 90 days. It captures recent trends — if a product is suddenly selling faster (or slower), the WMA reflects that sooner.

wma_velocity = sold_last_90_days / in_stock_days_last_90

Good for: spotting momentum shifts, seasonal patterns, or new product traction.

The system compares WMA to flat velocity to detect trends:

ratio = wma_velocity / flat_velocity
RatioDirectionMeaning
> 1.2Trending Up ↑Selling faster recently
0.8 – 1.2Flat →Stable demand
< 0.8Trending Down ↓Slowing down

When velocity lies: the low-confidence problem

There's a subtle trap in the velocity formula. Remember: velocity = total_sold / in_stock_days. But what happens when in_stock_days is very small?

The 3-Day Trap
Product A — 200 days of data
600 sold / 200 in-stock days = 3.0 units/day

200 days of evidence. This velocity is highly reliable. You can trust it to plan a 6-month order.

Product B — 3 days of data
9 sold / 3 in-stock days = 3.0 units/day

Same velocity number! But only 3 days of evidence. Those 9 units could be one wholesale order. This velocity is a guess, not a fact.

Both products show the same velocity (3.0 units/day). But Product A's estimate is built on 200 data points while Product B's is built on 3. If you order 6 months of stock for Product B based on this, you might end up with years of unsold inventory.

How the system handles this

The PO Builder flags any item with less than 14 days of in-stock data as "low confidence." These items get an amber warning badge showing how many days of data they have. When you see this flag, you should manually review the suggested quantity — the math might be technically correct, but the underlying data is too thin to trust at face value.

What should you do with low-confidence items?

▼

Options when you see a low-confidence flag in the PO Builder:

  • Reduce the order quantity — halve it, or order a conservative minimum
  • Wait for more data — if the product just came into stock, let it sell for a few more weeks before deciding
  • Use your judgment — you know the product. Is that velocity plausible? A niche calligraphy pen selling 3/day is suspicious. A popular canvas pad selling 3/day is normal.
  • Check the sales history — click into the SKU detail to see the actual transactions. Were those 9 units one big order or spread across multiple days?
Chapter 7

ABC/XYZ Classification

Not all products are equal. Some generate most of your revenue. Some sell predictably, others erratically. The ABC/XYZ system categorizes every SKU on two dimensions:

ABC — Revenue Importance

Based on the Pareto principle (80/20 rule):

ATop 80% of revenueYour stars
BNext 15% of revenueImportant
CBottom 5% of revenueLong tail

XYZ — Demand Predictability

Based on coefficient of variation (CV):

XCV < 0.5Stable, predictable
YCV 0.5 – 1.0Moderate variation
ZCV > 1.0Erratic, unpredictable

What is "Coefficient of Variation" in plain English?

▼

CV measures how wildly demand swings compared to the average.

The system buckets in-stock days into weeks, sums the demand per week, then calculates:

CV = standard_deviation / mean
  • Low CV (X): Sells roughly the same amount each week. Easy to forecast.
  • Medium CV (Y): Some weeks high, some low, but within a pattern.
  • High CV (Z): Huge spikes then nothing. One wholesale order of 500, then silence for weeks.

Needs at least 4 weeks (28 days) of in-stock data to compute.

Chapter 8 — Deep Dive

Lead Time, Buffers & The Reorder Formula

This is the most important chapter. We'll build the reorder formula from scratch, one concept at a time, so you see why each piece exists — not just what it does.

Part 1: Start with a fantasy world

Imagine a world where ordering is instant. You call your supplier, and the goods teleport into your warehouse the same second. In this world, you'd never need to plan ahead. You'd just wait until you have 0 units, press "order," and they're here.

Fantasy World Order Quantity
order_qty = whatever you want
No planning needed. Stock appears instantly. But this world doesn't exist.

Part 2: Welcome to reality — Lead Time

Lead time is the number of days between placing an order and receiving the goods. For Art Lounge, suppliers are overseas, so this is typically 90–180 days (sea freight).

This changes everything. Here's why:

The Lead Time Problem — A Story

Let's say you sell Winsor & Newton Cadmium Yellow. It sells 2 units/day, and you have 150 units in stock.

You think: "150 units, selling 2/day — I have 75 days of stock. I'll order when I get low."

You wait until day 50. You have 50 units left. You place the order.

The supplier says: "Great! Your shipment will arrive in 120 days."

But you only have 25 days of stock left (50 units / 2 per day). You'll be out of stock for 95 days before the shipment arrives.

The correct thinking:

"My lead time is 120 days. During those 120 days, I'll sell 2/day = 240 units. I need at least 240 units in my warehouse at the moment I place the order, or I'll run out before the shipment arrives."

The key insight

You're not ordering for today. You're ordering for future you — the version of you that exists 120 days from now when the shipment finally arrives. You need to predict how much stock that future version will need, and make sure the order covers it.

This is why the formula multiplies velocity by lead time:

Minimum Order (No Buffer)
minimum_need = velocity × lead_time_days
"How many units will I sell between now and when the shipment arrives?"
Visual: What happens during lead time
Day 0
ORDER PLACED
SHIPMENT ARRIVES
Day 120
During these 120 days, you keep selling:
2 units/day × 120 days = 240 units consumed
If you don't have 240 units when you order, you'll hit zero before the ship docks.

Part 3: Why the minimum isn't enough — Enter the Buffer

So far we've said: order velocity × lead_time. But there's a fatal flaw in this logic.

Velocity is an average. And averages lie.

If you order exactly 240, one extra dealer order could wipe you out.

This is the core problem: ordering exactly the average guarantees you'll run out roughly half the time. Sometimes demand will be below average (you're fine), sometimes above average (you're out of stock). For a business, even a 10% chance of stockout on a top product is unacceptable.

What the buffer actually does

The safety buffer is a multiplier that adds a cushion on top of the mathematical minimum. Think of it as insurance:

Buffer = Insurance Against Uncertainty
// Without buffer (risky) order_qty = 2 units/day × 120 days = 240 units // You're betting demand is exactly 2/day for 120 straight days. // Any spike = stockout. // With 1.3x buffer (safe) order_qty = 2 units/day × 120 days × 1.3 = 312 units // You have 72 extra units (30% cushion) to absorb: // - Demand spikes (a big wholesale order you didn't expect) // - Shipping delays (the vessel is late by 2 weeks) // - Supplier shortfalls (they shipped 90% of what you ordered) // - Seasonal surges (Diwali, back-to-school, etc.)
The analogy

Imagine you need to drive 120 km and your car gets 10 km/liter. The math says you need 12 liters. But do you fill up exactly 12 liters? No — you fill 15 or 16 just in case. Maybe you hit traffic (burn more fuel), maybe there's a detour (more distance), maybe the gauge isn't perfectly calibrated. The extra fuel is your buffer.

Part 4: Why different products get different buffers

Not every product deserves the same amount of insurance. The buffer should reflect two things:

How much does it HURT to be wrong?

This is the ABC dimension. Running out of an A-class product (top 80% of revenue) means losing real, significant money. Running out of a C-class product (bottom 5% of revenue) is a minor annoyance.

A High stakes Stockout = losing real money
B Medium stakes Stockout = noticeable loss
C Low stakes Stockout = minor issue

How LIKELY are you to be wrong?

This is the XYZ dimension. A product with stable, predictable demand (X) rarely deviates from the average. A product with erratic demand (Z) could swing 3x above the average on any given week.

X Stable Average is trustworthy
Y Moderate Average is okay-ish
Z Erratic Average is misleading

Now combine them. The buffer should be highest when:

  • The cost of being wrong is high (A-class product — lots of revenue at stake)
  • AND the chance of being wrong is high (Z-class demand — wildly unpredictable)

The Matrix, Explained Cell by Cell

ABC × XYZ Safety Buffer Matrix
X (Stable) Y (Moderate) Z (Erratic)
A 1.2x 1.3x 1.5x
B 1.2x 1.3x 1.4x
C 1.1x 1.2x 1.3x
A Z — Highest buffer: 1.5x

Your best-selling product with wildly unpredictable demand. Worst case scenario. Running out costs you the most money, AND you're most likely to mis-predict. Order 50% extra.

Real example: A popular canvas set that sells 1–2 per day normally, but a school order of 50 can come in any week. If you order the average, one big order wipes you out.
C X — Lowest buffer: 1.1x

A slow seller with perfectly stable demand. Low risk all around. Running out barely hurts revenue, AND the average is reliable. Just 10% extra is plenty.

Real example: A niche calligraphy pen refill that sells exactly 1 every 3 days, to the same 4 customers. Demand is a metronome.

Why not just use the same buffer for everything?

▼

You could use 1.3x for everything. But the cost is either:

  • Over-stocking low-value products: If you buffer a slow-selling C-class item by 50%, you're tying up cash in inventory that sits on shelves for months. That cash could be spent importing more of your best sellers.
  • Under-protecting high-value products: If you only buffer an erratic A-class item by 10%, one unexpected spike can cause a stockout on the product that generates the most revenue.

The matrix is about allocating insurance where it matters most. It's the difference between insuring a Ferrari and insuring a bicycle — you spend more to protect what's worth more, especially if the Ferrari is driven unpredictably.

Part 5: The complete formula, assembled piece by piece

Now that you understand each piece, let's assemble the full reorder formula step by step:

Building the Formula, Layer by Layer
1 Start with velocity
velocity = 2.5 units/day

"This product sells 2.5 units per day when it's in stock."

2 Multiply by lead time
2.5 × 120 days = 300 units

"During the 120 days I wait for the shipment, I'll sell 300 units. That's the bare minimum I need."

3 Multiply by safety buffer
300 × 1.3 = 390 units

"But demand fluctuates. This product is ABC=A (high value) and XYZ=Y (moderate variability), so I add a 30% cushion: 300 × 1.3 = 390."

4 Subtract what you already have
390 - 150 in stock = 240 units to order

"I need 390 total, but I already have 150 in the warehouse. So I only need to order the difference: 240."

The Complete Formula
order_qty = (velocity × lead_time × buffer) - current_stock
velocity — how fast it sells
lead_time — how long until shipment arrives
buffer — insurance for uncertainty
current_stock — what you already have

Part 7: The "Order Now" Window — How the system alerts you in advance

Here's the question you're probably asking: "Does this system just tell me when it's too late? Or does it actually warn me ahead of time?"

The answer: it warns you ahead of time. That's the whole point of the "Warning" status. Let's walk through exactly how.

The key idea

The system doesn't just compute "you have X days left." It compares that number against your lead time to create a proactive ordering window. The Warning status means: "If you order right now, the shipment will arrive just in time. This is your action window."

How the warning window is calculated

The system adds a warning buffer on top of the lead time to give you a comfortable ordering window:

Warning Window Calculation
warning_threshold = lead_time + max(30, lead_time × 0.5)
The warning zone starts at lead_time + half the lead time (minimum 30 days).
Concrete Example: 120-Day Lead Time Supplier
lead_time = 120 days warning_buffer = max(30, 120 × 0.5) = 60 days warning_threshold= 120 + 60 = 180 days // Decision logic: // More than 180 days of stock → OK (no action) // 120 to 180 days of stock → WARNING (order NOW) // Less than 120 days of stock → CRITICAL (already late!)

Why is the warning buffer set to 50% of lead time (minimum 30 days)?

▼

The warning window needs to be proportional to the lead time. Here's why:

  • A supplier with a 30-day lead time (domestic): warning buffer = max(30, 15) = 30 days. So you get warned when you have 30–60 days of stock. That's 1–2 months of runway to place an order — reasonable.
  • A supplier with a 120-day lead time (sea freight): warning buffer = max(30, 60) = 60 days. You get warned when you have 120–180 days of stock. That's 2 months of extra runway — appropriate because overseas orders are slower to negotiate and arrange.
  • A supplier with a 180-day lead time (distant manufacturer): warning buffer = max(30, 90) = 90 days. You get warned when you have 180–270 days of stock. Three months of buffer because these orders often involve negotiations, samples, and customs paperwork.

The max(30, ...) ensures that even suppliers with very short lead times still give you at least a 30-day warning window. You don't want the system staying green until the last possible second.

What about "must stock" and "do not reorder" overrides?

▼

Sometimes the math isn't enough. You can manually override the system's decision per SKU:

  • Must Stock: Forces items with no data or out-of-stock to show as "critical." Useful for new products you know you need but that don't have enough sales history for the engine to calculate velocity yet.
  • Do Not Reorder: Forces the item to "no data" status. Useful for discontinued items still in Tally that you don't want cluttering your order recommendations.

Can suppliers override the buffer?

▼

Yes. Each supplier (brand) can have a buffer override set in the Supplier Management page. If a supplier has a buffer override of, say, 1.4x, it replaces the ABC/XYZ calculated buffer for all SKUs from that brand.

This is useful when you know from experience that a specific supplier is unreliable (ships late, shorts orders, etc.) and you want a blanket higher buffer for everything from them, regardless of the individual SKU's demand pattern.

Part 9: The Missing Piece — "How Many Months Am I Ordering For?"

🐛
Gap Found During This Exercise
The system has no concept of "coverage period"

The current formula is: velocity × lead_time × buffer - current_stock. This orders enough stock to survive until the shipment arrives, but says nothing about how long the stock should last after the shipment arrives.

Ordering for 3 months vs 6 months vs 1 year produces very different quantities. Right now, there's no way to configure or even see this in the system.

Proving the problem with real numbers

Let's trace what actually happens when you follow the system's suggestion:

Scenario: Ordering When Critical (Most Common Action Case)
velocity = 2 units/day lead_time = 120 days (sea freight) buffer = 1.3x current_stock = 240 units (120 days left — just entered Critical) // What the system calculates: raw_need = 2 × 120 × 1.3 = 312 suggested_qty = 312 - 240 = 72 units // What happens 120 days later when the ship arrives: stock_at_arrival = 240 - (2 × 120) = 0 units ← just ran out shipment_arrives = +72 units new_stock = 72 units = 36 days of stock // Result: // You waited 4 MONTHS for a shipment... // ...and got barely 1 MONTH of stock. // You need to immediately order again. // And THAT order takes another 4 months. 🔴

The problem is clear: the formula uses lead_time for two different things:

What lead time SHOULD control

WHEN to order (the warning/critical thresholds). This part works correctly. The alert system is solid.

What's missing

HOW MUCH to order. The quantity should be based on "how many months of stock do I want after the shipment arrives?" — a separate coverage period parameter.

What the formula should look like

Corrected Formula (Proposed Fix)
order_qty = velocity × (lead_time + coverage_period) × buffer - current_stock
lead_time — days until shipment arrives
coverage_period — days of stock AFTER arrival
Same Scenario With 6-Month Coverage Period
velocity = 2 units/day lead_time = 120 days coverage_period = 180 days ← NEW: "I want 6 months of stock after arrival" buffer = 1.3x current_stock = 240 units // Total demand to cover: total_days = 120 + 180 = 300 days raw_need = 2 × 300 × 1.3 = 780 units suggested_qty = 780 - 240 = 540 units // What happens 120 days later: stock_at_arrival = 240 - 240 = 0 units shipment_arrives = +540 units new_stock = 540 units = 270 days = ~9 months! ✅ // Now you won't need to order again for months. // The next Warning would come at 180 days of stock, // which is about 3 months from now. Comfortable.

What coverage_period would look like in practice

Supplier Type Lead Time Coverage Period Total Order Window Reasoning
Sea freight (Europe) 120–150 days 180 days (6 months) 300–330 days Long lead time, order twice a year
Sea freight (Asia) 90 days 90 days (3 months) 180 days Shorter lead time, can order quarterly
Domestic supplier 30 days 60 days (2 months) 90 days Fast restock, order every couple months

The coverage period should be configurable per supplier in the Supplier Management page, just like lead time already is. Different brands have different ordering rhythms.

Why this exercise matters

This gap was found by walking through the reorder formula from first principles and asking "does this actually make sense for how we buy?" The system correctly tells you when to order. It just doesn't correctly tell you how much — because it doesn't know how many months of stock each order should cover. This is a high-priority fix.

Chapter 9

Nightly Sync: Putting It All Together

Every night, a single script runs the entire pipeline end to end. Here's exactly what happens:

1

Extract Master Data

Pull all stock categories (brands), stock items (SKUs), and ledgers (parties) from Tally. Upsert into PostgreSQL.

168 brands, 22,538 SKUs, 1,276 ledgers

2

Auto-Classify Parties

Run the 5-priority ruleset on any unclassified parties. Also detect new parties that appeared since last sync.

3

Sync Transactions

Pull all vouchers for the financial year from Tally (~190 MB XML, ~110 seconds). Parse inventory entries. Insert new transactions into the database.

~17,300 vouchers → ~92,000 inventory line items

4

Reconstruct Daily Positions

For each of 22,538 SKUs, replay every transaction day by day to produce daily stock positions. Uses the dual-anchor method.

Produces ~5.2 million daily position rows (22K items x ~240 days)

5

Calculate Velocities & Classify

Compute flat velocity, WMA velocity, trend direction, ABC classification, XYZ classification, and safety buffers for every SKU.

6

Compute Reorder Status

For each SKU: calculate days-to-stockout, determine status (critical/warning/ok), compute suggested order quantity. Roll up metrics to brand level.

Incremental Mode

The pipeline supports incremental computation: if only 200 items have new transactions, it only reconstructs positions for those 200. ABC/XYZ classification always runs for all items (since it's a relative ranking). This makes routine syncs much faster.

After the sync completes:

An email notification is sent reporting success/failure, counts, and any warnings (like new unclassified parties). The dashboard now shows fresh data for the morning team to review.

Reference

Glossary of Key Terms

SKU
Stock Keeping Unit. A single unique product. "Winsor & Newton Cadmium Yellow 37ml Tube" is one SKU.
Brand / Category
A group of SKUs from the same manufacturer. Tally calls these "Stock Categories." You have 168 brands.
Velocity
Units sold per day, averaged over in-stock days only. The core metric that drives everything.
Lead Time
Days between placing an order with a supplier and receiving the goods. Sea freight is typically 90-180 days.
Safety Buffer
A multiplier (e.g. 1.3x) applied to the order quantity to account for demand uncertainty. Higher for erratic products.
Days to Stockout
Current stock divided by velocity. How many days until you run out at the current selling pace.
Voucher
Tally's term for a transaction record (sale, purchase, credit note, etc.). Contains one or more inventory entries.
Party
A customer, supplier, or internal account in Tally. Each party is classified into a channel (wholesale, online, etc.).
Channel
The sales pathway: wholesale (B2B dealers), online (Magento/Amazon/Flipkart), store (retail counter), supplier, internal, or ignore.
WMA
Weighted Moving Average. Velocity calculated over only the last 90 days, giving more weight to recent trends.
Dual-Anchor Method
Our reconstruction technique: anchor on Tally's closing balance (backward) AND opening balance (forward) simultaneously for maximum accuracy.
Pipeline
The sequence of computations that transforms raw Tally data into reorder recommendations. Runs nightly via sync script.
