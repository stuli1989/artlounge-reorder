What counts as demand for velocity?

Not every Tally transaction is a real sale. Here's what's in vs out:

  • Wholesale outward — e.g., Hindustan Trading Co buys 72 units (25,036 units total this year)
  • Online outward — e.g., MAGENTO2 orders (22,099 units), Flipkart, Amazon
  • Store outward — Counter Collection sales (16,205 units across QR/CC/Cash)
  • Internal transfers — Art Lounge India moved 28,795 units between locations. Not customer demand.
  • Credit Notes — customer returns. Stock comes back in.
  • Physical Stock adjustments — inventory corrections, not sales.
  • Supplier purchases — ColArt shipped you 65,393 units. That's stock in, not demand.

We only count active days

What counts as "active"? A day is active if the item had physical stock (closing balance > 0) or if a real sale happened that day. Tally's books often show negative balances due to SKU renames and the company merger — but if someone bought it, the product was on the shelf. So we count those days too.
This is critical for Art Lounge. You import in bulk with 3-6 month gaps between shipments. Many items are out of stock for months at a time. Dividing by 365 would give you a much lower velocity and you'd order too little — guaranteeing another stockout.

Why do we add them together?

Each channel drains from the same physical stock pile. You don't have separate inventory for wholesale vs online vs store — it's all the same stock sitting in your warehouse.

Why track channels separately if we add them anyway? Because it tells you where your demand is coming from. If wholesale drops to zero but online doubles, the total velocity might stay the same — but it means something very different for your business. Channel breakdown helps you spot shifts in demand patterns.

The ship-vs-air trade-off

Sea freight: Cheaper per unit, but you wait months. Good for routine restocks where you plan ahead.

Air freight: Expensive, but gets here fast. Use when stock is critically low and you'll run out before a sea shipment arrives.
